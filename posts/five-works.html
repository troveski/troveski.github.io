Five Works of Art I Keep Thinking About

June 2026

I spent a year in Italy during my master's degree, and it changed something in me I didn't expect. Before that year, I had never cared about art — not once in my life. I walked past paintings in school trips without looking. I thought museums were for other kinds of people.

Then I was in Rome, and I had time, and I started going in. I'm not sure what flipped. Maybe it was just having nowhere else to be. But at some point I started feeling things in front of certain works — a stillness, a pull — that I hadn't expected to feel. These are five I still think about.

1. The Calling of Saint Matthew — Caravaggio

Contarelli Chapel, San Luigi dei Francesi, Rome

You walk into a small side chapel in a church near the Pantheon. It's dark, as these chapels tend to be. Then your eyes adjust and you see it on the left wall. A shaft of light cuts across a group of men counting money at a table. One of them — Matthew — points at himself as if to ask: me?

Caravaggio doesn't paint saints the way saints are supposed to look. These are real people, rough hands, ordinary clothes. The light is doing everything. Standing there, I understood for the first time why people have spent five hundred years talking about this man.

2. Saint Bartholomew — Rome

Rome

There is a statue of Saint Bartholomew that stopped me cold the first time I saw it. He looks almost skeletal — impossibly thin, every tendon visible. I thought it was a stylistic choice until I looked into it.

Bartholomew was martyred by being flayed alive. His skin was removed. The statue shows him carrying it — draped over his arm like a coat. What you're seeing isn't thinness. It's a body without its skin. Once you know, you can't unsee it. I stood there for a long time after learning that.

3. The School of Athens — Raphael

Stanza della Segnatura, Vatican Museums, Rome

The classic. The one everyone has seen reproduced a hundred times. I was skeptical it would still land in person. It does. The scale alone — the whole of classical philosophy gathered in one imagined hall — is something a screen can't give you. Plato pointing up. Aristotle gesturing down. Every figure in dialogue. It feels less like a painting and more like an argument.

4. The Martyrs of Gorkum — Cesare Fracassini

Rome

Nineteen priests and laypeople, arrested in 1572 in the Dutch city of Gorkum by soldiers who offered them a deal: renounce a few points of Catholic doctrine and go free. They refused. They were hanged.

Fracassini painted them in the moments before. What strikes me isn't the drama — it's the calm. These people had decided. There's something deeply unsettling and deeply moving about a face that has already made its peace. I don't share their faith, but I think about that kind of conviction often.

5. Equestrian Statue of Marcus Aurelius

Piazza del Campidoglio, Rome (original: Capitoline Museums)

Marcus Aurelius wrote the Meditations for himself — a private journal with no intention of publication. He wrote again and again about the vanity of fame, the smallness of reputation, the certainty that everything will be forgotten. He is now one of the most remembered people in human history.

I used to go to the Campidoglio just to sit with the statue. Not to think about anything in particular. Just to be near something that old, and that honest. There's a specific kind of company in a great Stoic.
